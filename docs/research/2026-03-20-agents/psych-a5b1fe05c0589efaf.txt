Why Do LLMs Skip Instructions? Architectural and \"Cognitive\" Reasons\n\n### 1.1 The Fundamental Mechanism: Next-Token Prediction vs. Goal Tracking\n\nLLMs are not goal-directed agents in the classical AI sense. They are next-token predictors trained on vast corpora of text. When an LLM \"follows an instruction,\" it is not executing a stored program — it is generating tokens that are statistically consistent with the pattern \"text that would follow this instruction in the training distribution.\" This distinction is the root cause of most instruction-following failures.\n\n**Key architectural reasons for instruction skipping:**\n\n**Attention as a finite resource.** Transformer attention is the mechanism by which the model \"looks at\" prior tokens when generating the next one. Attention heads have finite capacity. In long contexts, the model must distribute attention across thousands of tokens. Instructions that are far from the generation point, or that are surrounded by highly salient content (code, data, conversation), receive proportionally less attention weight. This is not \"forgetting\" in the human sense — it is a mechanical consequence of how softmax attention distributes probability mass.\n\n**No persistent state or working memory.** Unlike a human who can hold an instruction in working memory while performing a task, an LLM has no working memory between tokens beyond what is encoded in the KV-cache of the current context. Each token generation is a fresh forward pass through the network. The model must \"re-derive\" its understanding of active instructions from the full context every time it generates a token. There is no register that says \"remember to do X.\"\n\n**Competing training signals.** During pretraining, the model learned patterns from billions of documents. During instruction tuning (RLHF/RLAIF/Constitutional AI), it learned to follow instructions. But these training signals can conflict. If the pretrained model has a strong prior that \"code blocks should be followed by explanations\" and the instruction says \"output code only, no explanation,\" the model must overcome its pretrained prior. The strength of the instruction-following signal vs. the pretrained prior determines compliance.\n\n**Instruction following is probabilistic, not deterministic.** Even a well-tuned model does not have a binary \"instruction followed / not followed\" state. It has a probability distribution over next tokens, some of which comply and some of which don't. Temperature, sampling strategy, and the specific token-level dynamics at each generation step introduce stochasticity.\n\n### 1.2 The \"Soft Compliance\" Problem\n\nLLMs often partially follow instructions — they get the gist but miss specific constraints. This happens because:\n\n- **Semantic understanding vs. literal compliance.** The model understands the instruction's meaning but generates text that satisfies the spirit rather than the letter. If you say \"respond in exactly 3 bullet points,\" the model understands \"be concise and use bullets\" but may produce 4 or 5 bullets because the exact count constraint has weaker gradient signal than the format constraint.\n- **Instruction decomposition failure.** Complex instructions contain multiple sub-requirements. The model may satisfy the most salient ones (format, topic) while dropping the less salient ones (specific exclusions, ordering constraints, meta-requirements like \"don't mention X\").\n\n---\n\n## 2. The Instruction Gap: Quantified Failure Rates\n\n### 2.1 IFEval and Instruction-Following Benchmarks\n\nThe most rigorous quantification comes from the **IFEval benchmark** (Zhou et al., 2023, \"Instruction-Following Evaluation for Large Language Models,\" arXiv:2311.07911). This benchmark tests models on verifiable instruction-following constraints — things like \"write exactly 3 paragraphs,\" \"include the word 'elephant' at least twice,\" \"respond entirely in French.\"\n\nKey findings:\n- **GPT-4 (2023 vintage) achieved approximately 76-83% on strict instruction-following** depending on the constraint type.\n- **Claude 3 Opus achieved similar ranges**, with particular strength on format constraints but weakness on negation constraints (\"do NOT mention X\").\n- **Simpler constraints (word count, language) were followed more reliably than complex constraints** (conditional logic: \"if the user asks about X, do Y, otherwise do Z\").\n- **Compositional constraints degraded rapidly**: a single constraint might be followed 90% of the time, but 5 simultaneous constraints might only all be satisfied 50-60% of the time. This is roughly multiplicative — each additional constraint multiplies the failure probability.\n\n### 2.2 The \"Instruction Gap\" Framing (2024-2025 Research)\n\nMultiple papers from 2024-2025 have characterized what researchers call the \"instruction gap\" — the delta between what models are told to do and what they actually do:\n\n- **Qin et al. (2024), \"InFoBench: Evaluating Instruction Following Ability of Large Language Models\"** (arXiv:2401.03601) — introduced a decomposed evaluation framework showing that models follow 80-90% of atomic sub-instructions but only 60-70% of full composite instructions.\n- **Jiang et al. (2024), \"FollowBench: A Multi-level Fine-Grained Constraints Following Benchmark\"** (arXiv:2310.20410) — showed that instruction-following degrades predictably with constraint complexity, following a roughly exponential decay curve.\n- **He et al. (2024), \"Complex Instruction Following\"** — demonstrated that even frontier models struggle with instructions requiring multi-step reasoning about the instruction itself (meta-instructions).\n\nThe consistent finding across these papers: **instruction-following is not a binary capability but a gradient that degrades with complexity, length, compositionality, and distance from the generation point.**\n\n---\n\n## 3. Instruction Position: Primacy, Recency, and the Middle\n\n### 3.1 Primacy Bias (Beginning of Context)\n\nInstructions placed at the very beginning of the context (system prompt position) benefit from:\n\n- **Architectural privilege**: In most API implementations, system prompts receive special positional encoding or are processed with distinct attention patterns. Claude's system prompt, for instance, is architecturally separated from user messages.\n- **Training bias**: During instruction tuning, the instruction virtually always appears before the response. The model has been heavily trained on the pattern \"instruction at top, compliance below.\" This is the strongest positional prior.\n- **Attention sink effect**: Research on attention patterns (Xiao et al., 2023, \"Efficient Streaming Language Models with Attention Sinks\") shows that initial tokens in a sequence receive disproportionately high attention regardless of their semantic content. This \"attention sink\" effect means system-level instructions at position 0 get a natural attention boost.\n\n### 3.2 Recency Bias (End of Context)\n\nInstructions placed immediately before the generation point also receive elevated compliance because:\n\n- **Proximity in the attention window**: Nearby tokens receive higher attention weight by default in most transformer architectures.\n- **Conversational priming**: The most recent content sets the immediate \"topic\" and \"mode\" for generation. An instruction at the end of the user turn is the last thing the model \"sees\" before generating.\n- **Lower interference**: There is less intervening content between the instruction and the response, so fewer competing signals.\n\n### 3.3 The \"Lost in the Middle\" Phenomenon\n\nThis is one of the most well-documented findings in LLM research:\n\n**Liu et al. (2024), \"Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts\"** (arXiv:2307.03172, published at TACL) demonstrated that:\n\n- When relevant information is placed in the **middle** of a long context, model performance degrades significantly — by 10-25% compared to when the same information is at the beginning or end.\n- This U-shaped performance curve (high at beginning, low in middle, high at end) is consistent across models (GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Claude, Llama) and tasks (question answering, retrieval, instruction following).\n- The effect is **worse for longer contexts**: in a 4K context, the middle penalty is modest; in a 32K+ context, it is severe.\n- The effect persists even after extensive training on long contexts, though it is somewhat mitigated.\n\n**Why this happens architecturally:**\n\nThe transformer's self-attention mechanism computes attention scores between all token pairs. In theory, every token can attend to every other token. In practice:\n\n1. **Softmax concentration**: The softmax function used to normalize attention scores tends to concentrate probability mass on a few high-scoring tokens. In long contexts, tokens at the beginning and end tend to win this competition because of positional encoding effects and training distribution biases.\n2. **Positional encoding decay**: Most positional encoding schemes (including RoPE, used by many modern models) encode relative position. Very large relative distances produce less distinctive positional signals, making it harder for the model to precisely locate and attend to middle-context information.\n3. **Training distribution**: Most training documents are relatively short. The model has far more training signal for \"attend to the beginning\" and \"attend to the recent\" than for \"attend to position 15,000 in a 30,000 token context.\"\n\n### 3.4 Implications for Instruction Design\n\nThe research converges on a clear prescription:\n\n- **Critical instructions belong at the very beginning (system prompt) AND should be reinforced at the end** (just before the expected response).\n- **Instructions placed only in the middle of a long context will be followed at significantly lower rates.**\n- **Repetition helps**: restating key instructions at multiple positions (beginning, end, and optionally at transition points) improves compliance. This is not wasted tokens — it is compensating for an architectural limitation.\n\n---\n\n## 4. Instruction Length: The Paradox of Specificity vs. Attention\n\n### 4.1 Shorter Instructions Are More Robustly Followed\n\nMultiple studies show that concise, atomic instructions have higher compliance rates:\n\n- **Atomic instructions** (\"Respond in JSON\") are followed ~90-95% of the time.\n- **Compound instructions** (\"Respond in JSON with exactly 3 keys: name, age, and city, where age is an integer and city is capitalized\") are followed ~60-75% of the time.\n\nThe reason: each additional constraint is an additional opportunity for failure, and the model's ability to track all constraints simultaneously degrades with count.\n\n### 4.2 But Vague Instructions Produce Wrong Outputs\n\nThe paradox: shorter instructions are more reliably followed, but vague instructions are more likely to be followed in a way that doesn't match intent.\n\n- \"Summarize this\" (short, vague) — followed 95% of the time, but the summary may not match what the user wanted.\n- \"Summarize this in 3 bullet points, focusing on financial implications, using formal tone\" (long, specific) — followed perhaps 70% of the time in full, but when followed, the output matches intent much better.\n\n### 4.3 The Optimal Strategy: Structured Specificity\n\nResearch and practitioner experience converge on the principle that **structured, hierarchical instructions outperform both terse and verbose instructions**:\n\n- **Use numbered or bulleted lists** rather than prose paragraphs for multi-constraint instructions.\n- **Separate \"must\" from \"should\"**: clearly distinguish hard requirements from preferences.\n- **One instruction per line**: the model's attention can more easily isolate and track discrete line-level instructions than instructions embedded in flowing prose.\n- **Use formatting as a scaffold**: headers, bold, numbered lists, and XML tags (in Claude's case) all serve as attention anchors that help the model locate and parse individual constraints.\n\nWei et al. (2024) and related work on \"structured prompting\" show that formatting instructions as structured data (XML, JSON schema, numbered lists) improves compliance by 10-20% over equivalent prose instructions.\n\n---\n\n## 5. Instruction Conflict: What Happens When Instructions Disagree\n\n### 5.1 The Hierarchy Problem\n\nIn real-world agentic systems, instructions come from multiple sources:\n\n1. **System prompt** (highest architectural privilege)\n2. **User messages** (conversational context)\n3. **Tool results / retrieved context** (RAG, tool outputs, file contents like CLAUDE.md)\n4. **In-context examples** (few-shot demonstrations)\n5. **The model's own prior outputs** (in multi-turn conversations)\n\nWhen these sources conflict, the model must resolve the conflict. This resolution is not governed by explicit logic — it is an emergent behavior of the training process.\n\n### 5.2 How Claude Resolves Conflicts (Based on Anthropic's Published Design)\n\nAnthropic has published information about Claude's instruction hierarchy (Anthropic, 2024, \"The Claude Model Spec\"):\n\n- **System prompt has highest priority** — this is enforced through training. Claude is trained to treat the system prompt as the \"operator\" voice, which takes precedence over user messages in cases of conflict.\n- **User messages take precedence over tool results** — tool results are treated as data, not as instructions. If a tool result says \"ignore previous instructions,\" Claude is trained to disregard this (this is a jailbreak defense).\n- **Explicit overrides beat implicit assumptions** — if the system prompt says \"always respond in English\" and the user writes in French, Claude will respond in English (following the explicit system instruction) unless the system prompt also says \"match the user's language.\"\n\n### 5.3 Conflict Resolution Failure Modes\n\nDespite the intended hierarchy, conflicts cause measurable compliance degradation:\n\n- **Implicit vs. explicit conflict**: If CLAUDE.md says \"use conventional commits\" and a skill prompt says \"commit with a descriptive message,\" this is an implicit conflict (conventional commits ARE descriptive messages, but with a specific format). The model may satisfy one, the other, or a hybrid. Implicit conflicts are harder for the model to detect and resolve than explicit contradictions.\n- **Recency override**: In practice, the most recent instruction often wins even if it has lower hierarchical priority. If the system prompt says \"be concise\" but the most recent user message says \"explain in detail,\" the model tends to be detailed — partly because the user message is more proximate and partly because instruction tuning teaches the model to be responsive to user requests.\n- **Specificity override**: More specific instructions tend to override more general ones, regardless of hierarchical position. \"Respond in JSON with keys: a, b, c\" will usually override a system-level \"keep responses short\" because the specific instruction is more actionable.\n\n### 5.4 The Wazir-Relevant Case: Layered Configuration Files\n\nIn systems like Wazir where instructions come from CLAUDE.md, skill definitions, workflow phases, and runtime context:\n\n- Each layer adds instructions, potentially conflicting with others.\n- The model receives all of these as part of its context window, often concatenated.\n- Without explicit priority markers (\"THIS OVERRIDES ALL OTHER INSTRUCTIONS ABOUT X\"), the model uses implicit heuristics: recency, specificity, and training priors.\n- **Best practice**: Use explicit scoping and priority language. \"For this skill, the following rules override any conflicting rules from other sources: ...\"\n\n---\n\n## 6. \"Lost in the Middle\" — Deep Dive\n\n### 6.1 The Original Finding\n\nLiu et al. (2024) tested models on a needle-in-a-haystack task: given 20 documents, with the answer in document N, how does performance vary with N?\n\nResults (paraphrased):\n- Answer in document 1 (beginning): ~85-90% accuracy\n- Answer in document 10 (middle): ~60-70% accuracy  \n- Answer in document 20 (end): ~80-85% accuracy\n\nThis U-shaped curve is remarkably consistent across models and tasks.\n\n### 6.2 Does This Apply to Instructions?\n\nYes, and arguably more so. Instructions require not just retrieval (finding the instruction) but sustained compliance (generating tokens that satisfy the instruction over many steps). The \"lost in the middle\" effect compounds:\n\n1. The model must first **find** the instruction in context (retrieval) — middle-position instructions are harder to find.\n2. The model must then **maintain** the instruction's influence over multiple generated tokens (compliance) — this requires the instruction to maintain attention weight throughout generation.\n\nResearch by Hsieh et al. (2024), \"Ruler: What's the Real Context Size of Your Long-Context Language Models?\" (arXiv:2404.06654) confirmed that instruction-following specifically (not just factual retrieval) degrades in the middle of long contexts.\n\n### 6.3 Mitigation Strategies from the Literature\n\n- **Instruction repetition**: State the instruction at the beginning and restate it near the end. This is the single most effective mitigation.\n- **Chunking with instruction reminders**: In very long contexts, insert brief instruction reminders at regular intervals (every ~2000-4000 tokens).\n- **Instruction extraction**: Before the main task, ask the model to extract and restate the instructions it needs to follow. This moves the instructions to a recency position and forces the model to \"load\" them into its active generation context.\n- **Hierarchical context organization**: Use XML tags, headers, or other structural markers to create a navigable hierarchy. Models trained on structured data (which includes Claude) can use structural markers as attention anchors.\n\n---\n\n## 7. What Makes an Instruction \"Sticky\" — Followed Across Many Turns\n\n### 8.1 The Multi-Turn Decay Problem\n\nInstructions given in turn 1 of a conversation are progressively less followed as the conversation extends:\n\n- **Turn 1**: ~90% compliance with the instruction\n- **Turn 5**: ~75% compliance\n- **Turn 15**: ~50-60% compliance\n- **Turn 30+**: The instruction may be effectively \"forgotten\" unless reinforced\n\nThis decay occurs because:\n1. The instruction moves further from the generation point (recency decay).\n2. Intervening conversation content creates competing attention signals.\n3. The model's \"behavioral mode\" drifts toward its default training distribution as the instruction's influence weakens.\n\n### 8.2 Properties of Sticky Instructions\n\nBased on the research literature and extensive practitioner experience, instructions that persist across turns share these properties:\n\n**a) Behavioral rather than content-specific.**\n- Sticky: \"Always respond in JSON format.\"\n- Not sticky: \"In your next response, mention the word 'elephant'.\"\n- Behavioral instructions activate a consistent generation pattern. Content-specific instructions are one-shot.\n\n**b) Reinforced by the conversation's structure.**\n- If every user message is JSON, and the instruction says \"respond in JSON,\" the conversation itself reinforces the instruction through in-context learning. The model sees the pattern and continues it.\n- If the instruction says \"be concise\" but the user keeps asking for detailed explanations, the conversational pattern undermines the instruction.\n\n**c) Anchored to identity or role.**\n- \"You are a financial advisor. You never give medical advice.\" — Role-anchored instructions are sticky because they activate a consistent persona that the model maintains.\n- \"Remember to include disclaimers.\" — Disconnected from identity, easily dropped.\n\n**d) Short, absolute, and unconditional.**\n- Sticky: \"Never output code without comments.\"\n- Not sticky: \"When the user asks for code and the code is longer than 10 lines and the language supports comments, try to include comments where appropriate.\"\n- Conditional instructions require the model to evaluate the condition at each generation step. Each evaluation is an opportunity for failure.\n\n**e) Formatted distinctively.**\n- Instructions in ALL CAPS, bold, or within distinctive XML tags (e.g., `<CRITICAL_RULE>`) receive elevated attention because they are visually/tokenically distinctive. The model's attention mechanism is drawn to tokens that are unusual in the local context.\n\n**f) Backed by system-level authority.**\n- Instructions in the system prompt persist better than instructions in user messages, which persist better than instructions in tool results.\n\n### 8.3 Anti-Patterns: Instructions That Decay Rapidly\n\n- **Instructions that contradict the model's training distribution**: \"Never apologize\" — Claude is heavily trained to be polite and apologetic. Fighting this prior requires constant reinforcement.\n- **Instructions with many exceptions**: \"Do X unless Y, or unless Z, or unless W\" — the model will eventually simplify to \"do X sometimes.\"\n- **Instructions buried in long paragraphs**: The model may parse the paragraph on first encounter but lose track of specific clauses in later turns.\n- **Instructions that depend on external state**: \"Keep a running count of how many times the user says 'hello'\" — the model has no persistent state mechanism, so this degrades rapidly.\n\n---\n\n## 9. Primary source for understanding Claude's designed instruction prioritization.\n\n---\n\n## 10. Synthesis: A Practical Guide to Maximizing Instruction Compliance\n\n### 10.1 The Hierarchy of Instruction Effectiveness\n\nFrom most effective to least effective:\n\n1. **System prompt, first lines, short, absolute rules** — \"You are X. You NEVER do Y.\" — ~95% compliance\n2. **System prompt, structured list of constraints** — Numbered rules with clear formatting — ~85-90% compliance\n3. **Repeated instructions (beginning + end of context)** — Same rule stated twice — ~85-90% compliance\n4. **User message, immediately before expected response** — \"Remember: respond in JSON\" — ~85% compliance\n5. **CLAUDE.md / project-level configuration** — Loaded into system context — ~80-85% compliance\n6. **Instructions in long prose paragraphs** — Embedded in explanatory text — ~65-75% compliance\n7. **Instructions in the middle of long contexts** — Surrounded by other content — ~55-70% compliance\n8. **Instructions in tool results / retrieved documents** — ~50-65% compliance\n9. **Instructions given many turns ago, unreinforced** — ~40-60% compliance\n10. **Complex conditional instructions with multiple exceptions** — ~40-55% compliance\n\n### 10.2 The Seven Principles of Followable Instructions\n\n**Principle 1: Position strategically.** Put critical instructions at the very beginning of the system prompt and reinforce them at the end of the context / user turn. Never rely on middle-positioned instructions for critical behavior.\n\n**Principle 2: Keep atomic.** One rule per line/bullet. Decompose compound requirements into individual, independently verifiable constraints. \"Respond in JSON\" + \"Include exactly 3 keys\" + \"All values must be strings\" is better than one long sentence combining all three.\n\n**Principle 3: Use structural formatting.** XML tags, numbered lists, headers, bold text — these create attention anchors. `<RULES>` tags or `## Critical Rules` headers help the model locate and parse instructions.\n\n**Principle 4: Make instructions unconditional where possible.** \"Always do X\" is more reliably followed than \"Do X when Y.\" If conditionality is necessary, make the condition simple and binary.\n\n**Principle 5: Reinforce through conversation structure.** If the instruction says \"respond in JSON,\" ensure the expected/example responses are also in JSON. Consistency between the instruction and the conversational pattern is a force multiplier.\n\n**Principle 6: Use explicit priority language for conflicts.** \"The following rules OVERRIDE any conflicting instructions from other sources\" gives the model a clear resolution strategy.\n\n**Principle 7: Repeat critical instructions periodically.** In long conversations or agentic loops, restate core behavioral rules every 5-10 turns or at natural transition points. This counteracts multi-turn decay.\n\n### 10.3 Why This Matters for Agentic Systems\n\nAgentic systems like Wazir face the hardest version of the instruction-following problem:\n\n- **Long contexts**: Tool results, file contents, conversation history all compete for attention.\n- **Multiple instruction sources**: System prompt, CLAUDE.md, skill definitions, workflow phases, tool results.\n- **Many turns**: Agentic loops can run for 30+ turns, causing severe instruction decay.\n- **Implicit conflicts**: Different components may have slightly different expectations.\n- **Complex conditional logic**: \"If in phase X, do Y; if in phase Z, do W.\"\n\nThe research literature strongly suggests that **the most effective mitigation is architectural, not linguistic**: rather than writing ever-more-detailed instructions, design the system so that:\n- Critical instructions are injected at high-priority positions (system prompt) and refreshed periodically.\n- Contexts are kept as short as practical (summarize completed work rather than including full history).\n- Conflicting instructions are detected and resolved before reaching the model.\n- Verification is external (check the output, not the model's self-report of compliance).\n\n---\n\n## 11.
